Coping Calmly

Posted July 19, 2019July 19, 2019 Shruti

Taking the girls for outdoor play every evening is a routine for me now. And whenever I am out with them, I make it a point to whisper in their ears — “sharing is caring” and “greeting everyone is good” — to ensure that they gel with other kids well and make more friends. This allows other kids also to be a part of their gang of two. Trust me it’s tough to be a part of the Twin gang.

Recently I’ve seen them responding and connecting well to everyone. They’ve started waving to my fellow mom friends and even make friends with the kids a little older than their age group. They’re usually busy and occupied during this 1 ½ – 2 hours of outdoor play, affording me some time to finish my pending calls or chit chat with other fellow moms without any Mumma Mumma sounds in between. I just wave at them every 5 mins and throw a flying kiss with a wide smile.

So just when I had started feeling proud of my awesome parenting skills (I don’t wait for others to praise my parenting skills. I do it for myself in my head quite frequently) the shocker came last evening. It all started when I got them ready and we were in the play area sharp at 6pm. They pretended to be all energetic and excited, smiled at everyone and joined their gang for half and hour of uninterrupted cricket.

Avani spotted a bug and came running to me to report what she had seen.

“Mumma! There’s a small bug and it can fly.” She hollered.

We all laughed and smiled. Suddenly a fellow mom (who Avani is not very familiar with) said, “Beta, this bug can’t fly. It just crawls.” That simple comment was enough for Avani to get furious. She started crying at the top of her voice.

“Mumma this Aunty is not nice. She says the bug can’t fly, but it does fly.” she mumbled in between her sobs.

I was struggling to pacify her but everything was in vain. By this time, the little commotion had attracted Dhvani’s attention. She darted across from the other side and came near us.

“Why did you do this to my sister?” she asked angrily from the fellow mom.

“Aww. I didn’t say anything to you beta.” replied the lady softly.

With that Dhvani also burst into tears. Now I had both of them holding me tightly and crying at the top of their voices. To say that I was embarrassed would be an understatement. It’s hard to explain what my state of mind was at that time.

I somehow managed to carry them back home.

Aggression in kids is very common at this growing age. They use aggression when they figure that it’s the easiest way to get their point across. They are not able to verbalise their feelings and the anxiety and frustration comes out in the form of aggression. At times it could be for very silly reasons. The outburst can be in any form — screaming, hitting, biting, or kicking. I have witnessed this with my kids and with other kids as well.

So it becomes very important at our end to handle the situation gently especially when the kids are showing a not so gentle behaviour in public. From my self-introspection I concluded that there are a few areas where I need to work on myself to address such situations.

First of all, I need to have a good control on my temper and not feel embarrassed of the kids behaviour when such incidences happen. Although yesterday’s episode made me furious initially, I was able to control my reflex of shouting back at my kids.

The girls are too young to understand their own emotions. It’s just a convenient way for them to show their displeasure. Parents need to keep a tab on their own temperament and mood swings while handling kids in such situations.

Secondly it is very important not to judge the kids for their aggressive behaviour. Not every instance of their behaviour needs your approval. I felt I need to admit that this aggression is also a part of them growing up and my responsibility as a parent is to handhold them and guide them peacefully. They’re just kids and we should let them be. They have the right to behave like kids. Of course they need occasional guidance about the right and the wrong.

Immediate redressal is hardly required. It’s very important to encourage an apology but it Doesn’t have to be done then and there. I found myself probing Avani to say sorry immediately when she was not even settled and was still in tears. This irritated her even more. Maybe because I was embarrassed of her act. Not maybe, I was and this was wrong on my part. They have to be given time to settle down with their emotions.

It’s important to give them the understanding of a good and a bad behaviour as they are growing up. Also we need to emphasize on the consequences of both types behaviours. Appreciation on a good behaviour and taking away a privilege in case of a bad one works for me.

Stories is another effective too to give an understanding of a good social behaviour to the kids. Real time narration of an accepted behaviour in form of a story can be a good idea.

The instances like yesterday make me revisit my strategies in the quietness of the night when the girls are asleep. I lie down and do a post mortem of my day. I notice that few ideas work a most backfire. You need to keep rethinking creative ways without any unreasonable expectation that they would work for you each time.

It’s a long way to go. As Sadhguru observed —

Parenting is a 20 year project. Once you are into it, you can’t come out till the project is complete.
