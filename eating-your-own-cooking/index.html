Eating Your Own Cooking

Posted September 5, 2019September 17, 2019 Shruti

The picture is from the prize distribution ceremony last week. It was part of Onam celebrations in our apartment society. Its good to be a part of a community where people from varies cultures celebrate together which gives the kids a way to get a good exposure of various traditions.

As part of the Onam event, a drawing competition was held for kids belonging to various age groups. I got the girls enrolled for this since they have a keen interest in coloring and drawing. I thought it would be a good experience for them and more than that it will be easy for me to engage them for 2-3 hours on a holiday which is always a herculean task. So I got their bags ready and we arrived at the venue on time. The title for their age group was “My Garden.”

A 30 minute session of drawing and coloring at home is a part of our daily routine and they had been practicing My Garden for the past 2-3 days. The girls were well versed with all the garden elements. So as soon as the event started they started scribbling on their drawing pad. I don’t need to tell you that it’s unnatural for a 3-4 year child to sit patiently for an hour or more doing one particular thing. Which means to ensure they finish their task during the competition, I had to bribe them in between with chocolates and biscuits. Somehow the girls finished their scribbling and coloring, we packed our bags, I clicked a few pictures and we were back at home. My day so far was as expected and I was successful in my ultimate aim of engaging them for a good time.

Later during the day I got a call from my fellow friend who was also one of the organizers for the event that Avani bagged the first prize in her age group in the drawing competition. We were asked to be present at the venue in the evening for attending the prize distribution ceremony and collect the certificate. My friend also mentioned that the other twin Dhvani’s piece was also good but was finally not selected. I would be lying if I say that I didn’t jump with joy. Although I had enrolled the girls without any such expectation but this piece of news made me super happy. A mother’s joy you know. I called out for my husband in the other room and told him that Avani has stood first. We were all happy and joyous till Dhvani interrupted us.

“Mumma you always say coming 1st or 2nd is not important. To participate is important.” She said this with a grin on her face.

Before I could respond, came the second question from her as she grabbed my hand.

“Then why are you saying that only Betu (this is how she addresses Avani) has stood first? I also participated so I have also come first.”

I held her tight in my arms and said, “Yes. You both did well and you both have come first.”

“Then I will also be receiving a prize on the stage today with Betu?” she asked with a sparkle in her eyes. Yes was the only word I could utter. I looked at Avani from the corner of my eyes who was busy playing with her doll unaffected by the proceedings in the house. She did her job at the competition, got handful of chocolates and was sorted for the day.

Later before the prize ceremony I drove to a nearby store, picked up small gifts for the girls. At the event both the girls climbed up the stage hand in hand. Avani was handed over a gift and a certificate. I quietly requested the chief guest to handover the gift I had got for Dhvani. The lady on the stage was gracious enough to accept my request and understood immediately why I did so.

This experience light up a firework of thoughts in my head. As I continued reflecting on it, two things stood out.

First, whatever you tell your kids gets registered in their subconscious mind. So it becomes important to be aware of what are you telling them. And whatever you teach them or tell them, as parents you need to practice it. As in my case I keep repeating to the girls that “participation is important, coming 1st or 2nd is not.” I say this mostly to avoid the fights between them & also because I don’t want them to become a part of the unnecessary competitive rat race. When the time came, Dhvani applied this teaching practically and reminded me (in her own innocent way) that I need to practice what I preach & I need to eat my own Cooking 🙂

Second, it took my attention towards the conventional grading system we have in our education system. I feel it’s obsolete and it creates a culture of good and bad students. The kids who are motivated by grades complete their assignments with interest and get promoted and those who are not they just leave it midway and are called as low achievers. And that’s the root of academic discrimination. This is harmful for a child and can turn him against learning. So to facilitate real learning it’s important to do it with a backdrop of playful environment. When kids are taught in terms of conversation and narrative feedback, learning becomes an effortless byproduct of the process.

Teaching in itself is a counterproductive activity if the student doesn’t want to be taught. Recently, I was skimming through a book on child psychology called Summerhill and found something interesting —

Whether a school has or has not a special method for teaching long division is of no significance, for long division is of no importance except to those who want to learn it. And the child who wants to learn long division will learn it no matter how it is taught.
